Trade Friction Between U.S. And China -- What Happened And How Does It Affect The Two Sides?

What Happened So Far?

The U.S. announced it would implement a 25% tariff on 15 June 2018 on Chinese imports that had amounted to $50 billion in 2017. And China responded the same $50 billion will go to effect the same day in a tic-for-tat manner to this U.S. tariff action.

In reaction to the Chinese retaliation, the U.S. announced that it would impose 25 percent tariff on another $200 million worth of Chinese goods. In quick reply, China promised to respond with 25 percent tariff on $60 billion more of U.S. imports.

President Trump said in his 2016 compaign, “I will never support a trade deal that kills Americans jobs.”

Tariffs are additional taxes on imported goods. Trump, wanting to encourage the domestic industry, imposed tariffs, which can drive up the price of foreign goods and theoretically allow domestic products a better chance to compete. That's why Trump slapped tariffs on aluminum and steel from foreign countries. According to the data from Bureau of Labor, U.S. unemployment rate drops to 3.7% in August 2018, lowest in more than 10 years. Therefore, tariffs probably helped make more jobs in the U.S.

Position of China

Chinese Ministry of Foreign Affairs expressed that unilateralism and economic hegemony are unacceptable, and threatening and putting pressure will only be counterproductive. The door of the Chinese side to dialogue and negotiation has always been open.

Time To De-escalate

Tariffs often means retaliation and a resulting trading war. 2018 US midterm election is coming closer. Trade tension could become a major political drag. If countries continue to match the U.S. tic for tat with their own tariffs, consequently citizens would feel it even deeper in their wallets. it is better to de-escalate the tension of trade friction if a deal could be made by negotiation
